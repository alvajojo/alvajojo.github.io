Specific Aims

Mapping Brain Region-Specific Druggable Regulators of Blood-Brain Barrier and Neuroimmune Signaling

Project Overview

Stress-related neuropsychiatric disorders are a leading cause of disability worldwide, yet the mechanism by which peripheral immune signals access and influence the brain following chronic stress remains incompletely understood. The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is a critical interface for brain-immune communication, and emerging evidence indicates that chronic stress induces region-specific dysfunction and immune cell recruitment, particularly in stress-responsive regions such as the nucleus accumbens (NAc). While prior research has identified increased BBB permeability and neuroimmune activation in the NAc following stress, the molecular determinants that confer regional vulnerability and their potential as therapeutic targets remain poorly defined. Large-scale human transcriptomic resources, such as the Adult Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) Project, provide an unprecedented opportunity to define baseline gene expression differences across brain regions, offering insight into intrinsic molecular programs that may predispose specific regions to dysfunction. In parallel, the Illuminating the Druggable Genome (IDG) database systematically classifies genes by therapeutic tractability, including understudied proteins (identified in the database as Tdark/Tbio) that represent a novel, unexplored space of potential drug targets. GTEx and IDG have not been integrated to identify region-specific, druggable regulators of BBB and neuroimmune function. The overall objective of this proposal is to identify brain-region-specific, therapeutically actionable regulators of BBB and neuroimmune signaling by integrating human transcriptomic data with druggability annotations using machine learning. The central hypothesis of this proposal is that intrinsic, region-enriched gene programs include understudied, targetable regulators that shape BBB function and immune cell trafficking, and that these regulators can be identified through data-driven modeling. This project will develop a novel, integrative framework linking human brain-region-specific gene expression to druggable target discovery, ultimately addressing a critical gap in our understanding of BBB regulation in stress-related disorders. More broadly, this approach establishes a generalizable pipeline for uncovering hidden therapeutic opportunities within large-scale genomic datasets.

Aim 1: Identify brain region-specific gene programs using machine learning applied to human transcriptomic data from GTEx

Rationale: Brain regions differ in their susceptibility to stress-induced dysfunction, but baseline transcriptional features underlying this vulnerability are not well defined. Machine learning provides a powerful approach to identify genes that are functionally predictive of regional identity, rather than simply differentially expressed. Hypothesis: I hypothesize that brain regions known to be highly vulnerable to stress will exhibit unique baseline transcriptional features compared to those that are not. I will 1a) apply supervised machine learning models to gene expression data from the GTEx portal to identify genes that distinguish stress-relevant brain regions, such as the nucleus accumbens (NAc). In addition, I will 1b) characterize whether the novel generated region-enriched gene programs are associated with BBB function and communication with the immune system using gene ontology and pathway enrichment analysis.

Aim 2: Identify druggable and understudied regulators of BBB and neuroimmune signaling

Rationale: Many genes involved in BBB dysfunction and immune cell signaling are not readily targetable. Identifying novel, understudied druggable targets is essential for translating molecular insights into therapeutic strategies. Hypothesis: A prioritized list of previously underexplored drug targets will be generated from our novel region-specific gene sets. I will integrate the genes identified in Aim 1 with IDG annotations to classify targets by druggability and current development stage (Tclin, Tchem, Tbio, Tdark), as defined by the IDG. Priority will be given to targets that are a) predictive of brain region, b) relevant to BBB or immune pathways, and c) classified as druggable and understudied.
